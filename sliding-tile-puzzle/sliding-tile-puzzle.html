Sliding Tile Puzzle Algorithm

Intro

While playing a sliding tile puzzle I was annoyed how I couldn't find a simple way to solve it, or move any piece where I want without messing up the other ones. So as many people do I went to the Internet to see how to properly solve one. The first article I found did actually teach the right method, though I feel like it could be described neater, so that is why I created this page. Another reason I wanted to make this website was to try out the 98.css framework, since it looks really cool.

The algorithm

What I like most about this algorithm is how elegant it is. It works for any size, because how it works is that it keeps making the puzzle smaller, so it basically reduces a N×N puzzle to an N-1×N-1 puzzle, until N reaches 1. The original source called this a "Divide and Conquer" technique, while I would say the term "Recursion" captures the algorithm a lot better. The basic way the algorithm works is by solving the top row, then the left row, which removes a row and a column each of the puzzle, after which we repeat these 2 steps.

The hard part is then how to easily solve the top row and leftmost column. I will show you how to solve these with the steps shown below. Each step I will give an example slide puzzle where in yellow is the tile that has been moved in that step to the position in that step. The blank tiles are tiles that can be any number still. Part 1: Solving the Top Row

The hardest part of solving a tile puzzle is of course that moving a tile interferes with other tiles. Luckily such a problem only crops up in this algorithm after finish almost an entire row.

Sliding Puzzle Algorithm: Solving The Top Row.
  1.  Solve the top row apart from the 2 right-most tiles.

    ▯ ▯ ▯
    ▯ ▯ ▯ 
  2.  If the top-right tile is in the top row, move it a row lower and make sure it doesn't move to the top row again during the next step.
  3.  Move the second right-most tile to the top right corner.
    1 ▯ 2
    ▯ ▯ ▯
    ▯ ▯ ▯ 
  4.  Move the right-most tile to the tile below the top right corner.
    1 ▯ 2
    ▯ ▯ 3
    ▯ ▯ ▯ 
  5.  Move the second right-most tile to its place, then the right-most tile to its correct place.
    1 2 3
    ▯ ▯ ▯
    ▯ ▯ ▯

Part 2: Solving the Leftmost Column

Now to solve the leftmost row we basically follow the same steps as in Part 1, only vertically instead of horizontally. For the first step in our example we won't have to move any tiles, but if it were a 4×4 puzzle, we would still have to place some tiles in this step.

Sliding Puzzle Algorithm: Solving The Leftmost Row.
  1.  Solve the leftmost row apart from the 2 bottom tiles.
    1 2 3
    ▯ ▯ ▯
    ▯ ▯ ▯ 
  2.  If the bottom-left tile is somewhere in the leftmost column, move it to another column.
  3.  Move the second to last bottom tile to the left bottom corner.
    1 2 3
    ▯ ▯ ▯
    4 ▯ ▯ 
  4.  Move the right-most tile to the tile right of the bottom left corner.
    1 2 3
    ▯ ▯ ▯
    4 7 ▯ 
  5.  Move the second right-most tile to its place, then the right-most tile to its correct place.
    1 2 3
    4 ▯ ▯
    7 ▯ ▯

Part 3: Finishing up

Just keep following the steps in part 1 and part 2 until you get to the puzzle of size 3x3. You may find yourself getting in a bit of trouble once you get to part 2 of the algorithm for a 3×3 puzzle, you have to skip step 1 as there are only 2 tiles to place. After solving this part you are left with a 2×2 puzzle with only 3 tiles. This should basically solve itself.
